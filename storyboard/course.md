## Slide 07 — What Goes Into the Script
Slide-ID: 1S07
Template-ID: content-bullets
Slide-Title: What Goes Into the Script
Caption-Text: A great video update follows a simple five-step pattern.
On-Screen-Text: Strong video updates follow a five-part recipe. Practice it until it is automatic.
Image: A technician on the shop floor pointing at a brake rotor on a Taycan while filming on a phone. Clean uniform, name tag visible. Composition emphasizes a deliberate, taught moment.

Voiceover-INTRO: Strong video updates follow a recipe. Practice it once before you record — you will sound prepared, not rehearsed. Open with the customer's name and your name. Identify the vehicle and the service. Then point the camera at what you are explaining — let them see the worn part, the leak, the measurement. And always close with a clear next step. "I will call you this afternoon at three" is a next step. "Call us back" is not. Give them something to do.
Bullet-1: Practice once before you record — do not shoot from the hip on complex topics
Bullet-2: State the customer's name and your name at the start
Bullet-3: Identify the vehicle and the service you are performing
Bullet-4: Show what you are explaining — point the camera at the actual part
Bullet-5: End with a clear next step — when you will call, what you need them to do
Callout-Text: A video without a clear next step is just a status report — give the customer something to act on.

Voiceover-INTRO: Seventy-seven percent of Porsche customers prefer text updates — but texting professionally is harder than it looks. Four rules cover most of it. Explore each one.
Voiceover-TAB-Permission: Never text a customer who has not invited you to. Channel preference belongs to them. The first time, ask: "Is text okay for updates?" Document the answer in the system so the next person knows too.
Voiceover-TAB-Scope: Text is for short, factual updates. "Your Cayenne is ready for pickup." "I will call you at two." If the message needs explanation, or it is bad news, pick up the phone or record a video instead.
Voiceover-TAB-Timing: Only text during business hours unless the customer has specifically said otherwise. A nine PM text reads differently than a one PM text — even if the content is identical.
Voiceover-TAB-Tone: Write in complete sentences. Skip emojis. No ALL CAPS — it reads as shouting. Tone is the hardest thing to carry in text, so leave nothing ambiguous.

Voiceover-INTRO: Most digital mistakes are not big dramatic ones. They are small habits that slowly cost you credibility. Four show up most often. Click each card to explore.
Voiceover-CLICK-AllCaps: ALL CAPS reads as shouting — even if you only used it for emphasis. Excessive exclamation points and question marks land the same way. Use punctuation and capitalization the same way you would in a professional letter.
Voiceover-CLICK-Casual: Emojis, slang, and abbreviations can read as friendly with one customer and unprofessional to another. The Porsche brand standard is the higher bar. When in doubt, leave them out.

Voiceover-CLICK-Stalking: Digital stalking is when you email, call, and text the same customer about the same thing inside an hour. It is well-intentioned and it feels like overkill to them. Pick one channel and give the customer time to respond.
